## Principle Statement
Strategic focus does not emerge automatically; it must be protected through explicit exclusions, demand filtering, and expectation control.

## Why It Matters
Platform efforts attract broad requests, adjacent agendas, and locally reasonable exceptions. Without deliberate exclusions, the strategy is rewritten by incoming demand.
In practice, this protection is often carried by people who actively filter, translate, redirect, or refuse work before it distorts the platform path.

## What This Principle Protects
- service selection and concentration
- boundary defense
- reusable platform direction
- expectation stability
- protection against bespoke drift
- human firewall behavior in core leadership roles

## Typical Failure Shape
The effort starts with a coherent direction, but surrounding demand expands the scope until focus, reusability, and continuity collapse.
The formal strategy may exist, but no person or small leadership core consistently performs the human filtering needed to keep incoming work from rewriting that strategy in practice.

## Notes
This principle includes firewall patterns, service portfolio narrowing, and explicit decisions about what the effort will not do yet.
Here, `firewall` should often be interpreted as a human firewall: a role or small group that actively filters, reframes, redirects, or blocks strategically distorting demand before it reaches the platform team's core execution path.
