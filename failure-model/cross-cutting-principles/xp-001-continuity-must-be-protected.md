## Principle Statement
Platform Engineering change should be designed to continue, accumulate, and reinforce itself over time rather than depending on short bursts of progress.

## Why It Matters
Even when progress is slow, long-term continuation can compound into major organizational change. Failures that interrupt continuity therefore matter disproportionately.
This includes not only funding, sponsorship, and operating cadence, but also whether knowledge workers in the platform effort can stay motivated, protected, and able to continue without burning out, and whether the surrounding community continues to widen instead of stalling at a small core.

## What This Principle Protects
- sustained sponsorship from executives and managers
- sponsorship memory
- next-win continuity
- protected capacity
- reinforcement cadence
- durable adoption
- sustainable knowledge-worker motivation
- continued expansion of the surrounding community beyond the core group

## Typical Failure Shape
The effort reaches an early result, but support, memory, protection, or momentum decays before the next stage can build on it.
The work may also become too draining, too repetitive, or too weakly reinforced for platform-team members to keep investing judgment and energy in it over time, while the surrounding outer group stops widening and the effort collapses back into a small repeatedly burdened core.

## Notes
This principle should be used to justify keeping continuity-related failures explicit even when they appear across multiple steps.
