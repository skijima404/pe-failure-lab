## Symptom Statement
Platform Engineering practice does not become internalized within the platform team inwardly, so the team delivers platform work without consistently operating according to the product, adoption, and enablement principles it expects others to follow.

## Consequence
The platform may exist as an offering, but the team itself does not sustain the behavioral model needed to institutionalize Platform Engineering as a durable way of working.

## Observable Signals
- The platform team continues to behave primarily as a delivery or support function rather than as a platform product and adoption function
- Internal platform-team decisions do not consistently reflect product thinking, user empathy, enablement, or institutional adoption logic
- When asked how Platform Engineering has changed the platform team's own way of operating, the answer is weak, inconsistent, or superficial

## User Interpretation
Participants are likely to feel that the team is building platform capabilities, but not fully embodying the Platform Engineering model in its own operating behavior.

## Structural Impact
- The credibility of institutional change weakens because the provider team does not consistently model the intended practice
- Old delivery habits persist inside the very team meant to anchor the new way
- Institutionalization remains partial because internal operating change lags behind external platform rollout
