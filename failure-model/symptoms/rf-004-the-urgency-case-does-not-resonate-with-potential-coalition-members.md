## Symptom Statement
In conversation, the urgency case is expressed in ways that do not resonate with the people expected to join the guiding coalition. It is framed only in executive targets, abstract strategic language, or buzzword-driven enthusiasm, without making the issue feel relevant to them.

## Consequence
Potential coalition members do not feel why this change matters to them, so they do not join, do not stay engaged, or participate only superficially when coalition-building begins.

## Observable Signals
- When asked "Why should I join?", the answer returns to company targets, strategic language, or buzzwords
- The case is explained only through executive goals such as cost reduction, efficiency, or strategic alignment
- People understand the words being used but do not see why they personally should care or act

## User Interpretation
Participants are likely to interpret this as a people or communication problem, but the underlying issue is that the urgency has not been expressed in terms others can recognize as their own risk, burden, or opportunity.

## Structural Impact
- Coalition-building starts from a weak base of motivation and shared commitment
- Cross-functional support remains thin because the case for change feels external to key members
- The transition from urgency-building to coalition-building breaks, reducing momentum into the next Kotter step
