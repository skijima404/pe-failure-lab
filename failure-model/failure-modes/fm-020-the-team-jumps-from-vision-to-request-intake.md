## Failure Mode Statement
The team jumps directly from vision-setting into request intake or delivery response instead of defining an initiative path first.

This does not mean that all stakeholder follow-up meetings are bad.
Bounded discovery, clarification, and initiative-design follow-up can be healthy when they stay clearly separate from delivery intake or solution commitment.

## Context
This tends to emerge when the pressure to show movement is high, so responding to visible requests feels easier than shaping the sequence of change intentionally.

## Mechanism
- The team treats incoming demand as the next step after vision definition.
- Request response becomes the default operating mode before strategic sequencing is clarified.
- As a result, the initiative path is replaced by reactive work selection.

Signals that this failure mode is present:
- stakeholder-specific follow-up quickly becomes a queue of asks to satisfy
- solution shaping starts before initiative boundaries are defined
- the team implies future handling of exceptions, implementation details, or bespoke support without first defining the operating model

Signals that this failure mode is not necessarily present:
- follow-up meetings are explicitly framed as bounded discovery or product-management interviews
- the purpose is to clarify needs, boundaries, prioritization, or adoption conditions
- no implementation promise or delivery-style intake is being made

## Countermove
- Separate strategy formation from request intake, and do not treat them as the same phase.
- Ask what sequence of initiatives should happen before broad demand response begins.
- Reject early delivery motion if the team cannot explain why that motion belongs in the path.
- Explicitly label follow-up meetings as discovery, boundary clarification, or prioritization if that is their purpose.
- Distinguish "I want to understand your needs better" from "I am taking your request into the platform team's backlog."
